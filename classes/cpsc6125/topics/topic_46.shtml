Dr. J Dana Eckart: Advanced Operating Systems (CPSC 6125) - Dates and Time

  1. The popular way of designating dates (in the US) is ambiguous. The date 10/11/12 can mean:
    1. 11 October 2012 (and the year could be many different centuries, e.g., 1912).
    2. 10 November 2012
    3. 12 Novemeber 2010
    4. ...
  2. There is a wide variety of date formats around the world, making unambiguous dates difficult to achieve. This article shows a map of what many countries use, and some countries even use multiple formats!
  3. The US is also one of the few countries that uses a 12 hour clock almost exclusively. This leads to confusion about what time 12:00 is. While "noon" and "midnight" help, in common use there is still confusion about whether midnight of 1 Jan 1980 is at the beginning of that day, or the end. The use of a 24-hour clock removes the ambiguity.
    1. 12:00am = midnight = 0:00
    2. 12:00pm = noon = 12:00
  4. The ISO 8601 date (and time) format
    1. Based on the Gregorian calendar.
    2. Given in significant order (with zero padded months and days).
    3. E.g., Albert Einstein's birthdate is: 1879-03-14
    4. The use of hyphens reduces ambiguity so that dates like 20101220 aren't miinterpreted as 20 Nov 1220 (thinking the original was in little endian format, DDMMYYYY).
    5. Time uses zero padded hours, minutes, and seconds. E.g., 16:05:00 (for 4:05pm)
    6. Using ISO 8601 date/time formats:
      1. As version indications on documents is preferrable to ordinal numbering since the "effective" date is immediately obvious.
      2. As part of file and/or directory names makes it easy to see multiple file versions, and sorting provides easy access to the earliest and latest versions.
  5. Julian Dates
    1. Some draw backs of the Gregorian calendar are:
      1. It is difficult to determine how many days have elapsed between two dates.
        1. It's not a simple subtraction since months have different numbers of days.
        2. The month of February has an additional day in leap years (a year is a leap year if it is divisable by 4, unless it is divisable by 100, unless it is divisable by 400).
        3. The switch to the Gregorian calendar (from the preceeding Julian calendar).
          1. In predominatly Catholic countries Thursday, 4 October 1582 (Julian calendar) was followed by Friday, 15 October 1582 (Gregorian calendar).
          2. Some (predominatly Protestant) countries didn't officially adopt the Gregorian calendar until the early-1750s.
      2. Unless care is taken to indicate the calendar being used, dates between 1580 and 1755 can be off by as much as 11 days.
    2. The Julian calendar was introduced by Julius Caesar in 46 BCE. Whereas Julian periods (used to count years) were proposed by Joseph Scaliger in 1583, and the counting of days since the first year of the Julian period, proposed by John Herschel in 1849.
    3. Julian dates count the number of days since its epoch (noon on 1 Jan 4713 BCE in the Julian calendar is the start of day 0) and are counted relative to Terrestrial Time (TT) at the meridian of Greenwich [similar to UTC but not adjusted for the Earth's varied rotation rate].
    4. Julian dates are used by astronomers, which is why the date changes at noon (the beginning of the astronomical day). Furthermore, the time of the date is shown as a fractional day (e.g., 8:06am on 1 Jan 2013 CE would be denoted as 2,456,293.8375).
