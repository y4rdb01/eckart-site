Dr. J Dana Eckart: Advanced Operating Systems (CPSC 6125) - Unix History

  1. The following was compiled from the Wikipedia articles: History of Unix, Berkeley Software Distribution, GNU Project, SunOS, Linux, MINIX, Mach kernel, and Android.
  2. Bell Labs, frustrated by the size and complexity of Multics, but not the aims, slowly pulled out of the project. Their last researchers to leave Multics, Ken Thompson, Dennis Ritchie, Doug McIlroy, and Joe Ossanna, decided to redo the work on a much smaller scale.
  3. In 1969, on a little used PDP-7 at Bell Labs, a team of Bell Labs researchers led by Thompson and Ritchie developed a hierarchical file system, the concepts of computer processes and device files, a command-line interpreter, and some small utility programs.
  4. In 1970, Peter Neumann coined the project name UNICS (UNiplexed Information and Computing Service) as a pun on Multics (Multiplexed Information and Computer Services): the new operating system was an emasculated Multics.
  5. When the Computer Science Research Group at Bell Labs wanted to use Unix on a machine much larger than the PDP-7, Thompson and Ritchie traded the promise of adding text processing for a PDP-11/20 machine.
  6. In 1970, the Unix operating system was officially named and ran on the PDP-11/20. A text formatting program called roff and a text editor were added. All three were written in PDP-11/20 assembly language.
  7. The UNIX Programmer's Manual was published on 3 November 1971.
  8. The first commercial instance of Unix worldwide was installed in early 1972 at New York Telephone Co. Systems Development Center.
  9. In 1972, Unix was rewritten in the C programming language. resulting in a much more portable system, requiring only a relatively small amount of machine-dependent code to be replaced when porting Unix to other computing platforms.
  10. Under a 1956 consent decree in settlement of an antitrust case, AT&T (the parent organization of Bell Labs) was forbidden from entering the computer business. Unix could not, therefore, be turned into a product but, under the terms of the decree, Bell Labs was required to license its non-telephone technology to anyone that asked.
  11. AT&T made Unix available to universities and commercial firms, as well as the United States government, under licenses. The licenses included all source code including the machine-dependent parts of the kernel, which were written in PDP-11 assembly language.
  12. Versions of the Unix system were determined by editions of its user manuals. Versions 4, 5, and 6 were released by 1975, adding the concept of pipes, which led to the development of a more modular code base, and quicker development cycles.
  13. The University of California at Berkeley was one licensee. The first Unix system at Berkeley was a PDP-11 installed in 1974, and the computer science department used it for extensive research thereafter.
  14. In 1977 Bill Joy (and a later cofounder of SUN Microsystems), then a graduate student at Berkeley, started compiling the first Berkeley Software Distribution (1BSD), which was released on March 9, 1978.
  15. The Berkeley Software Distribution (BSD, sometimes called Berkeley Unix) was developed and distributed from 1977 to 1995.
  16. Many contributions to Unix first appeared in BSD releases, notably the C shell with job control and the addition of TCP/IP network code to the mainstream Unix kernel. The accompanying Berkeley sockets API is a de facto standard for networking APIs and has been copied on many platforms.
  17. SunOS is released in 1983 for SUN Microsystems computers (based on BSD with thanks to Bill Joy).
  18. The GNU (a recursive acronym meaning "GNU's not Unix") Project was founded in 1983 by Richard Stallman to develop a free (as in freedom) computing software stack based on Unix.
  19. In 1984, several companies established the X/Open consortium with the goal of creating an open system specification based on UNIX. Despite early progress, the standardization effort collapsed into the "Unix wars", with various companies forming rival standardization groups. The most successful Unix-related standard turned out to be the IEEE's POSIX specification, published in 1988 and soon mandated by the United States government for many of its own systems.
  20. MINIX (mini-Unix), created by Andrew S. Tanenbaum, is released in 1987 as an independent Unix-like system designed to run on IBM PCs. It is a rewrite of Unix to avoid copyright and trademark problems with AT&T.
  21. In April 1991, Linus Torvalds begins writing Linux as an improvement over MINIX.
  22. The Linux kernel is released in 1992 under the GNU license, it has grown to become a full-featured and stable Unix OS.
  23. Carnegie Mellon University released the Mach kernel in 1985, which is the basis for FreeBSD, released in 1993, and Mac OS X, released in 1999 (based on NeXTSTEP released in 1989).
  24. Android, based on the Linux kernel, is released by Google in 2008.
