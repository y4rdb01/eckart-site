Dr. J Dana Eckart: Programming Languages (CPSC 5135) - Discrete vs Continuous

  1. Time: Discrete time models/simulations look at either events (typically interactions between model elements) or fixed time intervals. Discrete event simulations determine when the next interaction will occur and then update everything to represent the model's state at that time. Fixed time intervals are exactly that, the simulation progresses from one time to the next using a fixed increment (e.g., seconds, minutes, hours, days, years).
  2. Space: Divides the physical aspects of the model into areas (or volumes) of fixed size. The spatial units may be quite large with repsect to the entities they might contain, however all that is known is that an entity resides in a spatial unit at a certain time (but not exactly where within the spatial unit).
  3. False Dichotomy: Ultimately (von Neumann) computers are discrete machines with finite resources, so all simulations are ultiamtely discrete in nature. As the time (and spatial) units get smaller, we more closely approximate a continuous perspective.
  4. Adaptive Techniques: Adjusting the time and spatial sizes to reduce the error on an as needed basis is a great way to bridge the gap as well as clearly see discrete vs continuous as a difference in degree rather than as a binary choice. A common technique is to calculate the model values based on the current choice (e.g., time interval) and then to divide it into half and calculate over each half interval. If the result over the two half intervals differs from that over the interval taken as a whole by a certain amount (either fixed or on a percentage basis), then the interval is halved (and the technique is applied recursively). Whether or not such adaptive techniques are reasonable/possible often depends upon the nature of the model.
