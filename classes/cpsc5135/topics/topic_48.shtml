Dr. J Dana Eckart: Programming Languages (CPSC 5135) - Modeling and Simulation

  1. Models are simplified descriptions of some real-world process.
  2. Describing models using a formalized system ensures that they are self-consistent (unambiguous) and facilitates discovery of gaps between the model and the real-world process it is intended to represent.
  3. Simplification makes the modeled process easier to understand and allow smaller versions to be created (either as computer programs or as physical implementations - often called scaled models) that can "run" the model (simulation).
  4. The results of simulations are used to predict what will happen in the real-world process. Sometimes this is done to avoid carrying out an actual (and often very expensive or time consuming) experiment (as in a new automobile or aircraft design) or to predict what will happen in a natural phenomenon over which we have limited control (such as weather forecasting).
  5. When real-world data corresponding to a simulation are available (as in weather forecasting), changes to the underlying model may be made to reduce the gap between simulation results and real-world observations. Note however, that there is often a point of diminishing returns. More complex models become less useful in helping to understand the real-world process and the tweaks to make the results gap smaller in some scenarios makes it larger in others.
  6. The best models are the ones that explain the vast majority of behavior across a wide variety of scenarios using the simplest descriptions (e.g., fewest rules).
  7. Examples:
    1. Weather forecasting - In 2015 NOAA used 13 kilometer wide cells to cover the Earth, with sizes of only 3 kilometers aroudn North America. [Science News, 2 May 2015]
    2. Huricane track forecasting
    3. Climate modeling - In 2015 NASA used a grid resolution over the Earth of 25 kilometers (simulating a period of 150 years). [Gizmodo.com, 14 June 2015]
    4. Galatic collisions
    5. Disease proliferation
  8. Simple models of complex dynamical systems can often give rise to emergent behavior.
    1. Simple rules and local interactions
    2. Give rise to complex behavior over time
    3. One characteristic of emergent behavior is that the result after some large amout of time isn't possible to predict (often even at the qualitative level) without actually carrying out all of the actions that would occur over that time period.
    4. Such systems are often extremely sensitive to their initial (or starting) conditions.
    5. For example, life is an emergent property of physics (via chemistry).
