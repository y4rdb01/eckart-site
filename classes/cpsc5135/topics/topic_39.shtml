Dr. J Dana Eckart: Programming Languages (CPSC 5135) - Goto Statements vs Structured Programming

  1. Goto Statements
    1. Enable a jump to another statement in an executing program. Jumps are not limited to only going "forward", but may also go "backward" (textually) in the code.
    2. This capability, when misused, can quickly give rise to programs that are very difficult to understand (and debug).
    3. Edsger Dijkstra's letter to the editor (of the Communications of the ACM): Goto Statement Considered Harmful
    4. Spaghetti code is the term commonly used to described the tangled mass of exeuction paths that can result from an undisciplined use of the goto statement. Such programs were difficult to understand and debug.
    5. Structured programming and the corresponding structured control constructs were developed starting in the 1960s, to ease the creation of correct and reliable programs.
    6. For whatever reason, pasta dishes have become an accepted way of describing several basic programming paradigms. Several of them are described here.
  2. Structured programming provides a single entry and a single exit point for a block of code. Forward jumps within the block, usually to the end, are often supported. However, jumps into the middle of a block of code, or "backward" jumps are not supported.
