CPSC 6129: Group Project

There is a (nearly) semester long group project as part of this course. The project involves taking a new language, learning how to write significant programs in that language, and presenting to the rest of the class (via classnotes and video based in large part on the experience of your group in learning and using the language) your group's:

  1. Tutorial(s) for programming in the language;
  2. Techniques and idioms for efficient use of the language's capabilities;
  3. Traps and pitfalls to be wary of when using the language;
  4. A critical analysis of the language (the good, the bad, and the ugly);
  5. Comparison of major features in the language to counterparts in other languages that members of the group are familiar with (e.g., from CPSC 5132, other courses, work experience); and
  6. Suggestions, if any, for how the language might be improved within its design objectives.
In order to help keep everyone on track (it's all too easy to postpone things) the below schedule must be adhered to (failure to do so will result in point deductions):
  1. [Saturday, 23 Aug] Student Experience:
    Post the list of languages you are fimilar with to the Yahoo group for this course. Include the name of the language, a link to a description of the language, and an indication of your knowledge level:
    Familiar
    Have read about the language and am familiar with its capabilities, but not able to pick up code and read it with complete understanding.
    Reader
    Able to read code for a significant program written in the language, but have never written a program on your own.
    Novice
    Have written simple (i.e., toy) programs in the language, and would be able to augment more complex programs that someone else had written.
    Advanced
    Have written significant (and non-trivial) programs in the language.
  2. [Wednesday, 27 Aug] Group and Language Assignment:
    Assignment of people (and languages) to groups will be posted by the instructor.
  3. [Sunday, 7 Sep] Language Install:
    Groups shall ensure that everyone in their team has downloaded, installed, and confirmed operation of their language system by executing at least one of the sample programs that (nearly?) all languages come with. A link to a "video" transcript of (compilation and) execution of such a program must be emailed to the instructor by each member of the group (you can post your video to youtube.com as "unlisted" if you don't want anyone else to see it - except those to whom you give the link).
  4. [Saturday, 20 Sep] Program Descriptions:
    Your group must email to the instructor a list of 3-5 program descriptions for review. These are programs that you feel are sufficiently significant to illustarte all of the major features of your assigned programming langauge. The descriptions should be detailed enough that you could give them to someone else as a programming assignment. Be sure to outline at the top of each program description which of your group's language features it is meant to illustrate.
  5. [Saturday, 4 Oct] Program Approvals:
    After (perhaps multiple rounds of) feedback from the instructor have been incorporated into the program descriptions, the instructor will approve which should be implemented by the group as part of its project. All approved programs should be incorporated into the group's presentation (though the group is free to include more - and probably smaller - examples within their presentation in order to focus on key issues more directly).
  6. [Monday of the week prior to your scheduled presentation] Presentation Notes:
    Email your instructor html versions of your notes suitable for posting on the course web site. These notes must be the basis for your video presentation, but should include more detail than you are likely able to cover in the video. Ideally other students in the class should print these notes and be able to make substantial annotations to them as a result of watching the video presentation. This deadline enables the instructor to make these materials available to the rest of the class in a timely manner.
  7. [Friday of the week proir to your scheduled presentation] Presentation Video:
    Email your instructor the youtube.com link to your presentation video(s). It is strongly recommended that you divide your presentation into more consumable chunks of 10-20 minutes each. Please given a short phrase that describes the topic of each video segment. This deadline enables the instructor to make these materials available to the rest of the class in a timely manner.
  8. [Sunday, 23 Nov] Approved Programs:
    Email the instructor the completed programs that were previously approved for your group. Be sure to include any instructions on how to (compile and) execute your programs, providing descriptions of the desired input along with sample input files.
  9. [Sunday, 30 Nov] Test Questions:
    Each member of the group should email the instructor a list of:
    1. 10 true/false questions;
    2. 10 multiple choice questions (with 5 choices for each question); and
    3. 5 short answer/paragraph questions of a more open-ended nature.
    These questions should be specific to the language assigned to your group. You must include what you feel to be the correct answer for each of your questions. The questions should not be trivial, but must be answerable based on your group's presentation (published notes and videos).
  10. [Saturday, 6 Dec] Assessments:
    Each member of every group should email the instructor their own assessment of every group's presentations (including your own group) as well as an assessment of the contributions of the members of their group toward their own group's work (including yourself).
